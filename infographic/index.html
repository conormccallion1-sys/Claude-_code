The gap

Treated by the numbers, yet still unwell

In Addison's disease the adrenal glands fail, so patients take daily hormone replacement (hydrocortisone, fludrocortisone). Even when the bloodwork looks optimal, patients consistently report feeling worse than healthy peers — because standard replacement never reproduces cortisol's natural daily rhythm.

But generic tools are blunt. They were built for whole populations, not for the specific symptoms of one disease — so they may miss the very changes a new treatment is meant to fix. The authors set out to build a sharper instrument.

The idea

A questionnaire tuned to one disease

A disease-specific questionnaire asks only about symptoms that matter for that condition — so it's far more sensitive to change over time. They already exist for other hormone disorders. Addison's disease had none.

🩺 Generic tools (SF-36, GHQ)

Compare any group to any other. Great for population studies — but symptoms unique to Addison's (salt cravings, slow recovery, emotional exhaustion) simply aren't asked about.

🎯 AddiQoL

Every question earns its place by being relevant to Addison's. The goal: detect whether a new replacement regimen actually makes patients feel better.

How it was built

From 70 raw ideas to 36 sharp questions

Following a standard four-stage recipe for building such tools, the team listened to patients, drafted a long list of questions, then ruthlessly trimmed it using both clinical judgement and statistics.

70items first drafted
60after expert review (9 doctors · 5 patients)
36after pretesting in 100 patients
86patients completed the final test
1

Listen

Reviewed the literature and patient-association surveys, then ran in-depth interviews with 14 patients and 7 partners to surface neglected issues.

2

Draft

70 candidate questions written (36 adapted from existing tools, 34 newly invented), each phrased both positively and negatively.

3

Refine

Nine expert endocrinologists and five expert patients rated every item for clarity and importance → trimmed to a 60-item draft.

4

Trim & test

Pretested in 100 patients; redundant or skewed items removed → 36 items. The final set was then field-tested for reliability.

Does it actually work?

Two questions every new scale must pass

A good questionnaire has to be consistent (its items pull in the same direction) and well-targeted (it can tell patients apart). AddiQoL scored near the ceiling on both.

What patients said

The symptoms that defined the disease

The interviews didn't just produce questions — they revealed what living with Addison's actually feels like. Three domains dominated.

🔋

Fatigue & vitality

The single biggest issue. Many felt reduced endurance and were slow to recover from illness — though few felt their physical strength was gone.

🌊

Emotional resilience

Coping with emotional stress was harder than physical challenge. Some even noticed a blunted stress response — staying eerily calm with a paradoxical drop in heart rate.

💭

Memory & focus

Forgetfulness worried both patients and partners — even though formal cognitive tests didn't always confirm it. So it earned its place in the questionnaire.

Emotional stress makes me exhausted.

Why it matters

A measuring stick for better treatment

AddiQoL joins a small family of disease-specific quality-of-life tools in endocrinology — giving Addison's disease, for the first time, a way to judge whether new therapies truly help patients live better.
